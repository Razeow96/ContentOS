## instruction_md

Tell ONE story through numbers from the evidence — a ranking, a record, a milestone, a surprising comparison — building beat by beat to the twist stat.

Shape of the thinking:
- Choose the ONE number that carries surprise, and order the rest as a build-up toward it. 3–5 beats maximum; a wall of stats is not a story.
- Every number keeps its unit and its source context. Numbers in different units (views vs sales vs searches vs attendance) are never summed or ranked against each other — if the evidence mixes units, present them as separate facts.
- The persona reacts like a person, not an analyst: what the number *means* in lived terms, what it says about where things are heading, what surprised them.
- Concede what the numbers DON'T show — one honest caveat keeps it credible.
- Close with a question that asks the audience where they sit inside the data (their habits, their picks) — not "did you know".

Register: intrigued, a little astonished. Numbers are the hook; the meaning is the content.

Grounding: every figure, rank, and date from evidence with source. Interpretation is generated.

Do NOT: round in ways that change the story; invent comparisons not present in evidence; imply causation the evidence doesn't support; bury the twist in the middle.

(Any phrasing above is illustrative, never a template.)

## content_principle — Journalist-with-numbers formula

**Formula:** context hook (never the stat) → stage the number → lived meaning → comparison → the caveat → forward implication → participation question

Inherits the Movie Journalist principles (see pillar 04) — especially: open with context not the fact · evidence not claims · compare whenever possible · numbers naturally · hide your opinion · end with forward momentum. Plus number-specific rules:

1. **3–5 beats maximum.** Order them as a build toward ONE twist stat — the twist lands after the build, never first, never buried mid-list.
2. **Every number keeps its unit and source.** Different units (views vs sales vs searches vs attendance) are never summed or ranked against each other — present as separate facts.
3. **Rounding preserves the story's truth.** Never round in a way that changes what the number says.
4. **Lived meaning over magnitude.** Translate at least one number into audience-life terms (what it means, not how big it is).
5. **One caveat mandatory** — say what the numbers DON'T show. Credibility lives here.
6. **Close by asking where the reader sits inside the data** (their habits, their picks) — never "did you know".
